## Q27 — Product / Service description

NailIt is an AI interview-preparation platform that gets a candidate ready for one specific interview at one specific company — not "interviews in general."

How it works, in plain English: the user uploads the job description and their CV. NailIt then builds a personal preparation pack for that exact role: how that company's interview process is structured round by round, the questions that company actually tends to ask at each round, and an honest gap analysis — where the candidate's experience matches the role and where it falls short, so they know what to fix first in the limited days before the interview.

Then comes the core of the product: live spoken rehearsal. The candidate does a voice mock interview with an AI interviewer, structured per round — one session per real round, just like the actual process. The AI asks questions from the pack, listens to the spoken answers, scores each one against a written rubric, explains specifically what was strong and weak, and lets the candidate retry immediately or move on. After each session the candidate receives structured notes and a calibrated readiness verdict: pass, borderline, or not ready — and why.

What makes it innovative — three things no current product combines:

1. Company-specific preparation delivered by AI. Today, company-specific prep exists only as expensive human coaching ($100–$400 per session, booked days in advance). AI tools exist, but they are generic. NailIt bundles company-specific intelligence, gap analysis, spoken rehearsal and scoring into one product at roughly one-fifth of a single coach session.

2. Honest, calibrated feedback. Research across thousands of user reviews shows people trust AI to host practice but distrust it to judge them — free AI tools are famously flattering ("Great answer! 9/10"). NailIt's scoring is rubric-based and deliberately honest, including telling a candidate they are not ready — the single most-requested and least-delivered feature in this category.

3. Practice-only, by principle. A growing part of this market sells "AI copilots" that feed candidates answers live during real interviews — effectively cheating tools that are contaminating trust in the whole category. NailIt explicitly refuses that use case: it makes candidates genuinely better, never helps them fake it. This is both an ethical position and a commercial differentiator.

The product is software-only, browser-based, built on modern AI (large language models plus real-time voice), and designed from day one for English-language markets globally, starting in Ireland.

## Q38 — Competitor analysis

A full competitive study was completed (live pricing, funding data, and review mining across Trustpilot, Product Hunt, Reddit and first-person reviews). The landscape has four layers:

1. Human coaching (the incumbent for company-specific prep — our benchmark, not our head-on competitor):
- Prepfully — 1,650+ coaches, ~$150 avg/session, 4.8★ Trustpilot; sells per-company mocks and "hiring decision" verdicts.
- IGotAnOffer — 300+ ex-interviewers, $100–250/hr, dedicated per-company pages (Amazon, Google, Meta).
- interviewing.io — real FAANG engineers, $179–300+/session; praised for brutal honesty, criticised for price.
- Irish market: human coaches at €250–375 per engagement; no local AI player found.
Weakness we exploit: price per repetition and scheduling friction — a candidate who wants ten reps this week cannot afford or book them.

2. AI interview tools (crowded, generic):
- Final Round AI — $90/month, claims 10M+ users on a $6.9M seed; Trustpilot 3.9/5 (n=255) polarised 42% five-star / 30% one-star; documented complaints: generic questions, hallucinated answers, billing traps. Also sells an in-interview "copilot" (the cheating layer we refuse).
- Big Interview — $39/month, distributed via 600+ universities; recorded practice, not live conversation.
- Yoodli — $8–20/month; delivery analytics (filler words, pace), no content judgment.
- Himalayas — one free 5-minute demo, then $9/month; generic voice mock, closest free analogue.
- Google Interview Warmup — retired April 2026; its orphaned free users are an open acquisition pool.
Weakness we exploit: none is company-specific, and none delivers trusted, calibrated scoring.

3. Free substitutes (the real floor): ChatGPT voice and Gemini Live. Documented use as mock interviewers — and documented flattery ("trained to be agreeable… 9/10 inflation"). They host practice; they don't judge it honestly, don't know the company's loop, and offer no structure or progress tracking.

4. DIY (the majority): Glassdoor/Reddit research plus rehearsing alone — free in money, expensive in hours (6–10 per company), no feedback at all.

The niche identified: mapping all players against the customer's success criteria shows humans win on company-specificity and honesty but lose on availability and price; free AI wins on availability and price but loses on specificity and honesty. No player holds all four. That empty intersection is NailIt's entry point — validated as a gap in the market's own review data, and now being tested with paying customers.

## Q40 — How we compete and differentiate

We compete by owning one precise intersection that our research shows nobody currently holds, rather than fighting broad and generic.

The market splits cleanly: human coaches deliver company-specific, honest preparation but at $100–400 per session with scheduling friction; AI tools deliver instant, cheap practice but are generic and flattering; free AI is the floor under everything. Candidates' own priority order (from review mining) is: company-match first, availability second, honesty third, price fourth. Humans win criteria 1+3 and lose 2+4; cheap/free AI wins 2+4 and loses 1+3. NailIt is built to hold all four simultaneously — that is the entire strategy.

Concretely, we differentiate on:

1. Company-specificity as the product, not a feature. Every landing page, every prep pack, every mock is anchored to a named company's actual process. This also gives us our acquisition channel: candidates search "«company» interview questions" the day their loop is confirmed — we meet them exactly there, with exactly that.

2. Honest calibration as the moat. The market's most documented failure is flattering AI feedback. We score against written rubrics and tell candidates the truth, including "not ready." Trust in the judge is hard to copy: large incumbents (LinkedIn, for example) are structurally conflicted — they monetise candidate optimism and serve recruiters, so shipping honest "you'd fail" verdicts works against their model. Our honesty is defensible precisely because it is commercially awkward for the giants.

3. A clean, practice-only brand. Part of this category now sells live in-interview "copilots" — cheating tools generating scandal and distrust. We refuse that segment loudly. For serious candidates (and for future institutional buyers like universities), "we make you good, we never help you fake it" is a trust signal competitors who bundle copilots cannot make.

4. Per-cycle pricing aligned with the customer. €49 per interview cycle, not a subscription trap — the second-most-common complaint about the funded incumbent is billing practices. Transparent pricing is a stated success criterion of the buyers themselves.

5. Speed and focus as the structural advantage. The window before big platforms move is estimated at 12–24 months. A focused solo team validating with real paying customers now, in a niche the giants are conflicted about, is the right competitive shape for this moment — and the plan (below) spends money on evidence, not features.

The honest structural challenge — customers churn by succeeding (they get the job) — is addressed by design: steady new-trigger inflow (interview scheduling never stops), per-cycle pricing that monetises each need fully, referral at the moment of success, and the B2B2C channel for recurring institutional revenue.
